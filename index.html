In 1925 the surrealists invented a parlor game. One person draws the head — a cat — folds the paper, passes it on. The next draws a skeleton torso. The third draws human legs in a skirt. Nobody communicates.

The game — Paris, 1925

The result is
always a monster.
That’s the point.

André Breton and the surrealists invented cadavre exquis — exquisite corpse — to produce the unplanned, the irrational, the beautiful accident. Cat head. Skeleton torso. Human legs in a skirt. Three people who never spoke while drawing.

They published these monsters in their manifestos. They were thrilled. The disconnection between panels was the entire mechanism. The absurdity was the art. Each artist folded the paper and passed it on — the fold was the rule, and the rule was the genius.

Your enterprise runs a version of this game every day. Finance builds AI tools without telling Creative. Creative builds without telling Operations. The paper moves between departments, always folded, never opened. Nobody ever unfolds it.

“The surrealists made their monster on purpose. You made yours by accident.”
